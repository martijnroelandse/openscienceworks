The Story So Far

Short impact summary

Robin DiAngelo's What Does It Mean to Be White?: Developing White Racial Literacy, published by Peter Lang in the Counterpoints series, has accumulated 130 citations and sits at the 89th percentile of comparable scholarly works, establishing it as a substantive reference point in whiteness studies and anti-racist pedagogy. Its reach extends well beyond traditional academic citation, with confirmed use across at least ten open courseware contexts spanning institutions from MIT OpenCourseWare to the Open University.

Full narrative

DiAngelo's volume addresses a deceptively direct question—what it means to occupy the social position of whiteness in a racially stratified society—and approaches it through the lens of racial literacy, a framework that foregrounds the learned, ideological, and often invisible dimensions of white racial identity. The book engages core concepts including racial formation theory, white supremacy as systemic structure, individualism as a mechanism for deflecting structural critique, and the role of narrative in both reproducing and potentially disrupting racial hierarchies. Situated within Peter Lang's long-running Counterpoints series, which now extends to well over 560 volumes, the work finds a natural home alongside critical curriculum theory and transformative education scholarship. Its intellectual network draws on prominent scholars including Gloria Ladson-Billings, William F. Tate, and Noël A. Cazenave, signaling its positioning within established traditions of critical race theory and equity-oriented education research.

The citation trajectory tells a story of sustained and growing uptake. After modest early engagement in the years immediately following publication, citations accelerated sharply from 2016 onward, reaching 22 in 2019 alone, with further growth in subsequent years. The citing literature is dominated by journal articles (nearly 75 percent of citing works), but also encompasses 14 dissertations and 12 book chapters—a distribution suggesting the volume functions both as a scholarly reference and as a formative text for emerging researchers. Citing venues span whiteness and education studies, multicultural education, progressive human services, and religious education, reflecting the book's capacity to travel across disciplinary communities united by concerns about race and social justice. Geographically, the strongest uptake is in the United States, but scholars in Sweden, Canada, New Zealand, Australia, and the United Kingdom have also engaged the work, pointing to international resonance in contexts where majority-white institutions grapple with racial equity.

The pedagogical footprint of the book is particularly pronounced. Confirmed appearances across ten open courseware contexts—including materials on decolonizing higher education, anti-racist teacher preparation, and the intersectional experiences of minoritized educators—indicate that the volume functions as a live teaching resource rather than merely a cited authority. The educational sector accounts for the overwhelming majority of citing institutions (84 out of roughly 100 identifiable sector affiliations), and the work has been adopted in healthcare and nonprofit training contexts as well, suggesting practical application beyond the university classroom. Its alignment with UN Sustainable Development Goal 4 (Quality Education) is strong, with a model confidence score of 0.88, reflecting the book's direct orientation toward transforming educational practice.

Across its citing community, the book functions primarily as a conceptual anchor—scite analysis finds it overwhelmingly mentioned rather than contested, consistent with its role as a foundational framing text rather than a source of empirical claims subject to direct replication or refutation. The concentration of citations remains relatively distributed across institutions (an institutional concentration index of 17.4 percent), indicating broad adoption rather than a narrow cluster of enthusiasts. Taken together, these signals portray a work that has moved fluidly between scholarly synthesis, graduate training, and practitioner-facing pedagogy, occupying a durable and cross-sectoral place in ongoing conversations about race, identity, and the conditions under which white racial literacy can be developed and taught.
