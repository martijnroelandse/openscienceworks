Full narrative

Published in 2006 by Purdue University, Sisters in Science presents an important collection of narratives from 18 pioneering Black women scientists. Through oral histories, the book captures their personal journeys, emphasizing the intersections of race, gender, and scientific ambition. These stories span multiple disciplines, from engineering to microbiology, contributing valuable sociological and historical perspectives to biomedical and broader scientific fields. The authorship by Diann Jordan reflects long-term dedication, filling a significant gap in documented experiences of Black women in science.

The book’s thematic richness is underscored by its concept labels encompassing medicine, race biology, gender studies, and sociology, among others. These interdisciplinary foci demonstrate the work’s breadth in addressing not only scientific achievements but also systemic barriers and cultural contexts. Its oral history format grants agency and visibility to voices historically marginalized in academic discourse. This qualitative contribution complements quantitative scientific literature by providing nuanced insights into how identity shapes scientific careers and leadership roles.

Abstract:
Author Diann Jordan took a journey to find out what inspired and daunted black women in their desire to become scientists in America. Letting 18 prominent black women scientists talk for themselves, Sisters in Science becomes an oral history stretching across decades and disciplines and desires. From Yvonne Clark, the first black woman to be awarded a B.S. in mechanical engineering to Georgia Dunston, a microbiologist who is researching the genetic code for her race, to Shirley Jackson, whose aspiration led to the presidency of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, Jordan has created a significant record of women who persevered to become firsts in many of their fields. It all began for Jordan when she was asked to give a presentation on black women scientists. She found little information and little help. After almost nine years of work, the stories of black women scientists can finally be told.
